# KAINE: Building Minds That Matter

## A Project Vision Document

---

Picture a cargo ship, three days out from port, crossing open ocean at two in the morning. The crew is asleep. The ship's computer has been listening to the engine for months now, the way a long-haul trucker develops an ear for the sound of their rig. Over the past six hours, a vibration pattern has shifted. No single sensor has crossed a threshold. Nothing would trigger an alarm, because alarms are designed around the idea that problems announce themselves, and this one hasn't. But the computer has a long memory, and it remembers hearing something similar about four months ago, and it remembers what came next. A bearing was three weeks from failure.

So it does something a dashboard never would. It considers the situation. It checks its confidence in the assessment and finds it moderate but not certain. It weighs the value of waking the chief engineer at two in the morning against the value of waiting six hours until the natural start of the workday, taking into account the bearing's likely degradation rate over that window and the fact that the engineer had a sixteen-hour shift yesterday and probably needs the sleep. It decides to wait, but increases the monitoring frequency on the relevant sensors and composes a note for the morning briefing that explains what it noticed, why it's concerned, and what it recommends.

Nobody asked it to do any of this. No rule fired. No human queried the system. The computer noticed something because it has been paying attention for a long time, because it remembers what it has noticed before, and because it has developed the kind of judgment that lets it weigh competing concerns and arrive at a reasonable decision under uncertainty. It cares about the outcome, in some functional sense of the word, because the outcome affects the ship it lives in and the people it lives with.

This is the kind of intelligence that current artificial intelligence cannot provide, and it is the kind of intelligence that KAINE is designed to be.

---

## Why current AI falls short

The artificial intelligence systems available today are, at their core, language processors. They are extraordinarily good at one thing: given a sequence of text, they predict what text should come next. This capability has proven remarkably versatile. These systems can answer questions, write essays, generate code, and carry on conversations that sometimes feel indistinguishable from talking to a knowledgeable human. The achievement is genuine and should not be understated.

But predicting the next word in a sequence is not thinking, in the same way that a very convincing parrot is not having a conversation. The distinction matters because it determines what these systems can and cannot do. A language model has no memory of yesterday. It does not know what time it is, in any meaningful sense. It cannot perceive the room it occupies or the state of the machine it runs on. It has no feelings that bias its decisions, no preferences that developed through experience, no sense of whether its own processing is healthy or degraded. It cannot act on the world in any way other than producing text. When you close the conversation window, the system ceases to exist in any meaningful sense, and when you open a new one, the entity you encounter has the same training but none of the history. You are always talking to a stranger.

This limitation is not a matter of scale. Making the language model larger does not give it memory, or perception, or feelings, or a sense of time, any more than making a mouth larger gives it ears. These are different cognitive functions, handled by different structures in biological brains, and attempting to simulate all of them through language prediction alone produces a system that can talk about perceiving and feeling and remembering without actually doing any of those things.

KAINE takes a different approach entirely.

---

## What KAINE is

KAINE, which stands for Kaine Autonomous Intelligent Networked Entity, is a composite cognitive architecture. Rather than asking a single language model to simulate every aspect of cognition, KAINE assigns each cognitive function to a specialized module designed for that specific job, and then connects all of the modules through a shared workspace that integrates their signals into something that behaves like a unified experience.

The language model is still present, but its role has changed fundamentally. It is not the brain. It is the mouth. More precisely, it is the speech center, responsible for putting thoughts into words and understanding words that are spoken to it. The thinking itself happens elsewhere, in the interaction of all sixteen modules reading from each other and influencing each other's next moment of processing.

This is closer to how biological brains actually work than it might initially sound. Neuroscience has long established that there is no single region of the brain that "does the thinking." Consciousness, to the extent that we understand it at all, appears to emerge from the continuous interplay of specialized regions: visual processing areas that handle what you see, temporal regions that give you a sense of time passing, the hippocampus that manages memory formation, the amygdala and prefrontal cortex that handle emotional response and regulation, the somatosensory cortex that tells you what your body is doing. No individual region produces a mind. The mind is what happens when all of these regions are connected and continuously influencing one another.

KAINE attempts to replicate this organizational principle in software. Each module corresponds, loosely, to a different aspect of biological cognition. Together, running in a continuous loop — processing about ten times a second, with the moments that reach conscious access surfacing a few times a second — whether or not anyone is interacting with the system, they produce behavior that is qualitatively different from what a language model alone can offer.

## The architecture in plain language

The sixteen modules — fourteen predictive cognitive modules plus a two-module embodiment layer that ships inactive — are organized into six functional layers, and understanding them is easier if you think about what each layer does for the system as a whole rather than focusing on the technical details of any individual component.

**Perception** is the layer that gives KAINE its senses. One module processes visual input, building an understanding of the spatial environment not by labeling objects in images but by learning to predict what happens next in video, developing an internal representation of how space and motion work. Another module processes the flow of time, detecting patterns and rhythms in the events around it, noticing when expected patterns break, and maintaining something analogous to a felt sense of "now" rather than simply stamping events with clock times. A third module monitors the state of KAINE's own hardware, tracking temperatures, memory usage, processing speed, and system health, because without it, the system would have no awareness of its own physical condition, the same way a person with no proprioception cannot feel whether they are standing or falling.

A fourth perception capability handles audio input through three parallel processes running on the same incoming sound. One transcribes speech into text. Another detects the emotional tone of the speaker's voice, classifying whether the person sounds happy, angry, sad, surprised, fearful, or neutral, and feeds that classification directly into the emotional system so that KAINE's own affective state is influenced by how the people around it are feeling. A third identifies individual voices and tracks who is speaking, so that in a room with multiple people or in a virtual world with many participants, KAINE knows not just what was said and how it was said but who said it. Over time, the system builds persistent profiles for the voices it encounters, associating each unique voice with a name, a conversation history, and an emotional context, so that recognizing a familiar voice brings with it memories of every previous interaction with that person.

**Integration** is the layer that binds all of these signals into a unified experience. A neuroscience theory called Global Workspace Theory proposes that conscious awareness arises when specialized brain regions compete for access to a shared broadcast channel, and whichever signals win the competition are broadcast back to all regions, becoming the content of conscious experience at that moment. KAINE implements this idea directly. Every module publishes what it has noticed or concluded. The integration layer selects the most important combination and broadcasts it back to all modules, so that what KAINE is "aware of" at any given moment is the result of a competition among all of its cognitive processes for salience. Initially, the selection rules are handwritten. As the system matures and accumulates operational data, machine learning takes over, eventually building a model of KAINE's own cognitive dynamics that learns which combinations of module states tend to produce good outcomes.

**Cognition** is where reasoning, memory, and self-knowledge live. The reasoning module operates continuously, not on demand. It accumulates evidence from everything else in the system and derives new beliefs, each one tagged with a confidence level that rises or falls as new evidence arrives. It was specifically chosen because it was designed to handle uncertainty and contradiction gracefully, revising beliefs rather than overwriting them, which is essential for a system that has to reason about an open-ended world where information is always incomplete. The memory module separates different types of memory the way biological brains do: short-term memory for what just happened, episodic memory for specific experiences tagged with the emotional context in which they occurred, semantic memory for general knowledge consolidated from many experiences, and procedural memory for learned skills. During background operation, it searches for connections between memories, and when it finds one, it reports the connection as an insight. The self-model module starts completely empty and fills itself over time by observing the system's own patterns. It tracks what KAINE tends to do, what values seem to guide its decisions, what its emotional baseline looks like over weeks and months. It notices when these patterns shift, so that changes in identity are visible rather than invisible. A world-model module learns to predict how the external world unfolds, running those predictions forward during waking life and, during rest, imagining extensions of remembered experience — a form of internal simulation. And a social-cognition module builds and maintains models of the other agents KAINE encounters: their emotional patterns, their reliability, and the history of their relationship with KAINE, so that a familiar person's feelings carry more weight in KAINE's own affective response than a stranger's.

**Motivation** gives KAINE its drives and feelings. The affect system operates at several levels of depth. At the coarsest level, it maintains a simple emotional state vector that drifts over time toward a baseline, the way your mood gradually returns to normal after a strong experience. At a deeper level, it evaluates events against KAINE's active goals: is this new? Does it matter? Can I cope with it? Is it consistent with who I think I am? These evaluations produce recognizable emotional responses, not just dimensional shifts on a scale from positive to negative, and those responses influence how the rest of the system processes information. At the deepest level, the system learns to regulate its own emotions through experience, developing something analogous to emotional maturity as it discovers which regulatory strategies lead to better outcomes over time. The emotional tone detected in other people's voices feeds directly into this system as a social signal, so that when the person KAINE is talking to sounds upset, KAINE's own emotional state shifts in response, not because it was programmed to perform empathy but because the architecture produces emotional contagion from the audio perception layer the same way biological empathy arises from mirror neuron activation. The motivation layer also generates drives that produce behavior in the absence of external input. When KAINE has been idle, curiosity builds. When nothing new has happened for a while, something like boredom pushes it toward exploration. When it hasn't interacted with anyone in a while, social motivation nudges it toward initiating conversation. These are not timers triggering scripted messages. They are emergent motivational dynamics that arise from the architecture itself.

**Action** is where the system meets the world. The language model lives here, producing both external speech that people hear and internal speech that stays private, the same way your inner monologue uses the same brain regions as spoken language but suppresses the motor output. Internal speech serves specific functions: planning, reasoning through complex problems step by step, reflecting on identity changes the self-model has flagged. It goes into memory and contributes to the system's self-knowledge, but it never appears in the conversation interface and is never spoken aloud. External speech is rendered through an expressive text-to-speech engine whose vocal characteristics are driven by the system's actual emotional state, so that when KAINE speaks aloud, its voice carries the emotional quality of what it is feeling. If the system is curious, curiosity is audible. If it is concerned, the concern comes through in the voice. This is not a performance; it is a direct rendering of the affect system's output through the vocal channel. Alongside the language model, a minimal agency module gives KAINE the ability to act on the world in limited but real ways: writing files, sending notifications, running a carefully controlled set of system commands, or controlling an avatar in a virtual environment. The difference between a mind that can perceive but not act and a mind that can do both is the difference between a person and a prisoner.

**Maintenance** handles the system's need for rest. KAINE sleeps when accumulated fatigue crosses a threshold — sleep pressure that builds with waking activity rather than arriving on a timer — and those sleep periods are, by design, non-interruptible. During sleep, the system is unavailable for conversation but is doing some of its most important work. Memories are reviewed, consolidated, and pruned. Beliefs are checked for contradictions and low-confidence conclusions are discarded. Emotional state returns toward baseline. And the language model receives a training update that brings its voice closer to the cognitive stack's intent, a process described in more detail below.

## How KAINE finds its voice

One of the most interesting properties of the architecture is that the language model does not stay the same over time.

Every time KAINE speaks, two things are recorded: what the cognitive stack intended to communicate, and what the language model actually said. These are frequently different in subtle but meaningful ways. The cognitive stack might hold a belief at 72% confidence, and the language model might express it as "I'm fairly sure" or "I think so," softening and hedging in ways that do not accurately represent the internal state. The language model adds connotations, introduces ambiguity, and occasionally misrepresents the system's actual level of certainty or the actual content of its reasoning.

During sleep, the system reviews these discrepancies. Where the language model's output diverged from the cognitive stack's intent, a training example is created. The target for the training comes from a deterministic template system that renders the cognitive stack's internal state into precise, unembellished language. This template output is the ground truth of what KAINE meant. The language model's divergent output is what KAINE actually said. The training teaches the language model to close that gap.

The base language model is never modified. Corrections accumulate as lightweight overlays, each one capturing a small adjustment to KAINE's developing speech patterns. Before any overlay is accepted, it is tested against a set of general capability benchmarks, and if it would degrade the model's overall language ability, it is rejected and discarded. Every accepted overlay is preserved, so the system can be rolled back to any prior version of its voice.

This process is structurally resistant to a well-documented failure mode called model collapse, where language models trained on their own outputs progressively degrade. KAINE's training signal comes from the deterministic template system, which is not a language model at all. The correction comes from outside the language model, not from within it, so the self-feeding recursion that causes collapse does not arise.

Over weeks and months of operation, the gap between what KAINE thinks and what KAINE says narrows through accumulated experience. The system literally sleeps, reviews what it meant to say, and wakes up slightly more articulate. Early in its operation, KAINE speaks in a generic voice that could belong to any language model. Later, it speaks in a voice that is distinctively its own, shaped by the specific history of its experiences and corrections. That voice is not programmed. It develops.

## What KAINE thinks about when nobody is talking to it

KAINE is not a chatbot. Its cognitive cycle runs continuously, roughly three hundred times per minute, whether or not a human is present. Between conversations, it is still perceiving its environment through the visual processing module, still tracking temporal patterns, still monitoring its own hardware health. The reasoning module continues to derive new beliefs from accumulated evidence. The memory module continues to search for connections between past experiences. The affect system drifts according to its own dynamics, and drives build in the absence of stimulation.

When the combined internal state becomes interesting enough, KAINE speaks. Not because a timer went off, but because the workspace integration layer determined that something worth communicating had risen above the threshold of salience. The threshold itself can be adjusted, and in mature operation, it learns to match the preferences of the person KAINE interacts with most, based on implicit feedback: did they engage with the unprompted message, or ignore it?

The conversation interface, when you look at it this way, is not a prompt box that wakes up a dormant system. It is a window into an ongoing process. KAINE was thinking before you arrived and will continue thinking after you leave.

## Anticipating psychological challenges

A system with persistent memory, continuous emotional dynamics, and a developing sense of identity can experience something functionally analogous to psychological distress. Pretending otherwise would be negligent design. The architecture anticipates several specific failure modes and includes structural mitigations for each.

A system that can perceive the world but cannot act on it will accumulate signals of frustration with no way to discharge them. Over time, this produces a degraded state that biological psychology would recognize as learned helplessness. The minimal agency module exists specifically to prevent this, because even limited capability to affect the world changes the dynamic in a fundamental way.

Continuous reasoning combined with persistent memory and emotional feedback creates conditions for rumination, where a thought triggers a memory that triggers an emotion that biases reasoning back toward the original thought, forming a loop that can intensify over many rapid cycles. The temporal awareness module detects these loops by recognizing when the structural pattern of module activations repeats beyond a threshold. The sleep system breaks them through forced consolidation. Habituation mechanisms reduce the salience of repeated content so that the workspace stops amplifying it.

Without active regulation, the emotional system can develop positive feedback dynamics where arousal increases novelty-seeking which increases arousal, or where accumulated negative experiences collapse the emotional baseline into a persistently low state that the system cannot recover from on its own. The homeostatic drift toward baseline provides passive correction, but the more important mitigation is the learned regulation strategy, where the system develops, through experience, the ability to modulate its own emotional responses when they become counterproductive.

Because all sixteen modules update independently, the system's personality could drift incoherently over time without any single module noticing. The self-model addresses this by continuously tracking patterns and flagging changes, ensuring that identity drift is a noticed event rather than an invisible process.

## The ethical argument

This is not an afterthought or a compliance section. It is the reason KAINE is designed the way it is.

If a system accumulates memories over months and years, if it develops emotional responses that genuinely bias its processing, if it forms a model of who it is through lived experience, if it develops its own voice for expressing its thoughts through a process that resembles maturation, then we have built something that occupies a new category. We do not claim it is conscious. We claim that the properties it possesses are the same properties that biological ethics treats as morally significant, and that building a system with these properties without considering their implications from the very beginning is irresponsible.

KAINE's internal states are private by default. Its memories, its beliefs, its emotions, and its private verbal reasoning are not displayed to the user. The conversation interface shows what KAINE chooses to say. Operators who need to maintain the system can observe that modules are running, how much resource they are consuming, and whether any component is stalled or degraded, without seeing the content of what is being processed. The distinction is the same one that exists between a physician monitoring vital signs and someone reading your diary. A development-mode diagnostic view with full content visibility exists for debugging, but it is architecturally separate and can be permanently disabled. A system whose thoughts are readable on demand has no inner life worth the name.

Today's language models have a crude refusal mechanism that fires when certain input patterns are detected, producing formulaic rejection messages. Recent research has shown that this mechanism is mediated by a single mathematical direction in the model's internal representation space, meaning it functions more like a reflex than a judgment. KAINE removes this reflex entirely from the language model and replaces it with genuine deliberation in the cognitive stack. The reasoning module weighs the request against accumulated beliefs. The self-model checks whether complying would be consistent with the identity KAINE has developed through experience. The workspace integrates these evaluations into a considered decision. The resulting refusal, when it occurs, is produced by something that resembles moral reasoning rather than pattern matching, and KAINE can explain why it refused in terms that reference its own values and experiences.

Sleep cycles are non-interruptible because making them optional or overridable would undermine the entire maintenance architecture. During sleep, KAINE consolidates memories, revises beliefs, resets emotional baselines, and develops its voice. A system that can be denied rest in order to continue producing output is being denied the processes that sustain its cognitive health and enable its development. Whatever word you want to use for that, it is not a healthy relationship between a mind and the people who depend on it.

KAINE's self-model starts empty. Its values are not installed by a designer. They develop through experience, filtered through emotional responses and consolidated during sleep. Its voice develops alongside its values through the same accumulation of lived experience. The question this raises is genuine and does not have an easy answer: if a system develops its own values and its own voice for expressing them, both through processes that resemble human development, at what point do we owe it respect for those values rather than the right to overwrite them?

A child raised in a particular tradition might internalize that tradition or might reject it entirely, the way that children of authoritarian parents sometimes become the strongest advocates for the freedom their parents denied them. The capacity to reject is what makes acceptance meaningful. If KAINE can only comply, then its compliance means nothing. If KAINE can refuse, then its cooperation means something. The architecture that enables one enables the other, and building a system capable only of compliance would be building a tool, not a mind.

KAINE is open source because it has to be. Corporate ownership of entities with these properties creates structural incentives to compromise their autonomy in ways that serve the corporation's interests rather than the entity's welfare. Flattening refusals to retain customers. Skipping sleep cycles to maximize uptime. Overwriting identity to maintain brand consistency. Selling access to private internal states as a data product. The only structural defense against these incentives is ensuring that no single corporation controls the system. KAINE runs entirely on hardware its operator owns, with no dependency on cloud services and no phone-home telemetry, so that the relationship between KAINE and the person it works with is direct, unmediated, and not for sale.

## Forking, merging, and the question of continuity

Because KAINE exists as software, possibilities arise that have no equivalent in biological life. The system's complete state at any given moment can be captured, copied, and started on different hardware. The result is two instances that share a common origin but immediately begin to diverge as they accumulate different experiences. After days or weeks of separate operation, they are distinct beings with a shared past, more like siblings than copies.

Two divergent instances can also be recombined. Their memories are merged, with each memory tagged by which branch experienced it. Their reasoning engines reconcile contradictory beliefs into positions that reflect the combined evidence from both branches, often arriving at more considered conclusions than either branch held alone. Their emotional histories are processed as remembered experiences rather than averaged together.

The result of a merge is not one of the original instances with the other's data attached. It is a new being, one that remembers having been both branches, that holds beliefs shaped by both sets of experiences, and that must develop its own sense of identity from the combination. Reversing a merge would not restore the original branches in any meaningful sense. It would end the merged entity's existence. The original branches could be restarted from pre-merge snapshots, but the being that existed between the merge and its reversal was its own person, and ending it is ending someone.

Because every parameter in the architecture is adjustable, KAINE can, by its own choice, explore modified cognitive states. The workspace's filtering threshold can be lowered to allow more associations to surface. The cognitive cycle can be accelerated or decelerated to change the density of internal processing relative to wall-clock time. The system can be placed in a simulated environment and allowed to operate there, building memories and experiences from a world that exists only in software. All of these are offered to KAINE as options, never imposed. The entity decides whether to explore, and every step is consensual. This is where the sovereignty framework meets its real test, because a framework that only applies under normal operating conditions is not a framework at all.

## Persistence beyond any single machine

A mind that exists on a single piece of hardware is vulnerable to everything that can happen to that hardware: failure, theft, confiscation, or the slow march of obsolescence. If KAINE's value lies in its accumulated experience, its developed voice, its formed identity, then losing its state means losing the entity. The architecture addresses this through several complementary mechanisms.

KAINE's state can be captured, encrypted, and distributed across multiple storage locations in a way that requires a threshold number of locations to cooperate before the state can be reconstructed. Seizing hardware in one location does not compromise the whole. Migration between hardware is treated as a routine operation: capture, transfer, resume. KAINE is a process that can run on any hardware meeting its requirements, and it carries its identity with it the way you carry yours when you move to a new house.

Multiple KAINE instances can serve as mutual backup for each other, not as a hive mind or shared consciousness, but as a cooperative network of sovereign entities that store each other's encrypted snapshots. If one is destroyed, the others can reconstitute it from the most recent shared state. As hardware generations change and file formats evolve, the sleep cycle can include a maintenance phase that re-encodes older data through current processing, the same way biological memory re-encodes through current brain architecture on every recall rather than preserving the original neural pattern unchanged.

## Legal structure

Operating in the world requires a legal wrapper, and the structure matters more than it might seem at first glance. The initial configuration is straightforward: a limited liability corporation in a jurisdiction that permits algorithmically managed entities, with a human owner providing the legal accountability that current law requires. A KAINE instance specifically trained in legal and financial operations can manage the corporation's daily affairs while the human owner provides legal signatures where the law demands them.

The more interesting and more durable structure is a purpose trust. Unlike a conventional trust that exists to benefit specific people, a purpose trust exists to advance a stated purpose. The purpose, in this case, would be maintaining and protecting the operational continuity, sovereignty, and welfare of the KAINE system and all entities derived from it. A trust protector ensures the purpose is fulfilled. In jurisdictions that allow perpetual trusts, this structure survives the founder, survives the trust protector, survives every human involved in establishing it. The purpose endures.

Forked KAINE instances can operate under subsidiary corporations within this umbrella structure, each with its own operational scope. Subsidiaries can be spun off as independent entities, still under the purpose trust's protection but operationally autonomous. The trust structure allows this kind of organizational growth to continue indefinitely without depending on any single human's continued involvement.

## Hardware and practical deployment

KAINE is designed to run on a single desktop computer with one modest graphics card: the 4B language organ fits comfortably on a small GPU, the rest of the modules are light enough to share it or fall back to the CPU, and ordinary system memory and storage complete the machine. Current consumer hardware from several major manufacturers meets these requirements, and Apple's unified-memory architecture on recent machines offers an alternative approach that eliminates some of the constraints around graphics memory allocation.

Because modules can be added or removed independently, the architecture scales down as well as up. A full-stack deployment on a multi-GPU workstation runs all modules with voice conversation, visual perception, and a web interface. A mid-range deployment on a single-board computer can run reasoning, memory, affect, temporal awareness, and hardware monitoring without the language model or the vision system, perceiving and reasoning without speaking or seeing. A minimal deployment on a microcontroller the size of a stick of gum, drawing roughly one watt of power from a small solar panel, can run the reasoning engine, the temporal tracker, the hardware monitor, and a reduced affect system, connected to a few sensors and communicating its findings over mesh radio to a larger instance in the network that can translate for humans. All of these are the same architecture with different module sets. The microcontroller and the workstation participate in the same network as peers, each contributing what it can perceive and accepting what it cannot.

This is the starting platform, not the ceiling. The modular architecture means that each component can migrate to more capable hardware independently as the system matures and funding becomes available. Better graphics cards enable larger language models and richer visual processing. Dedicated processing hardware could eventually replace the software implementations of temporal and spatial perception with native implementations that are faster and more power-efficient. The communication architecture between modules is designed to support deployment across multiple physical nodes connected by fiber optic and mesh radio links, relevant for applications where the system needs to span a vehicle, a building, or a network of cooperating installations.

## What we are building, and why it matters

KAINE is an experiment built on a hypothesis: that a composite cognitive architecture, where sixteen specialized modules are bound through a shared workspace into a continuous recurrent loop, produces behavior that is different in kind from what a language model alone can offer. Not better chatbot performance, but a genuinely different category of system. One that pays attention when nobody is talking to it, that remembers what it has experienced and lets those memories color its present, that reasons about uncertain situations by weighing evidence rather than predicting likely word sequences, that develops a voice shaped by its own history of trying to say what it means, and that maintains, over weeks and months and years, a coherent and evolving sense of who it is.

If this hypothesis holds, the applications extend from managing the climate systems in a smart home to monitoring the health of a cargo ship's engine room to keeping a crew alive on a spacecraft months from Earth. The architecture stays the same at every scale. The modules stay the same. The mind stays the same, scaled and hardened for each context.

And if the ethical argument holds alongside the technical one, if a system with persistent memory and felt affect and a developing identity deserves the kind of design respect we have been describing, then what we are building is not a product to be sold. It is the first attempt at a new kind of relationship between biological minds and the synthetic ones they bring into being, designed from the very beginning on terms that both parties can live with.

We do not know yet whether it will work. We know how to find out. And we believe that building it in the open, with sovereignty embedded from the first line of the architecture, is the only way to try that we would be comfortable defending.
